1. Problem Definition & Target Users

The Indoor Navigation Challenge

Indoor navigation presents unique challenges compared to outdoor wayfinding. While GPS works well outdoors, it fails inside buildings due to signal attenuation. For people with low vision or seniors with mobility challenges, navigating unfamiliar indoor spaces‚Äîhospitals, universities, shopping malls, office buildings‚Äîcan be daunting and sometimes dangerous [1].

Why Doors Matter as Landmarks

Doors serve as critical landmarks in indoor environments for several reasons:

üö™ Doors as Navigation Anchors

  • Transition Points: Doors mark transitions between rooms, hallways, and spaces
  • Destination Markers: Finding the right door often means reaching your destination
  • Orientation Cues: Door locations help users understand spatial layout
  • Safety Exits: Emergency exits are critical for safety and evacuation
  • Accessibility Features: Doors often have room numbers, signs, and accessibility markers

Unlike walls or floors, doors have distinctive visual features: rectangular shapes, handles, frames, and often contrasting colors. These features make them theoretically detectable by computer vision systems [2].

Target User Groups

1. People with Low Vision

According to the World Health Organization, approximately 285 million people worldwide have visual impairments. For this population, mobile door detection can:

  • Provide audio feedback when approaching a door
  • Indicate door position (left, right, center) via spatial audio
  • Identify door handles and opening mechanisms
  • Read door signs and room numbers using OCR
  • Warn about obstacles near doorways

2. Seniors with Mobility Challenges

Older adults often experience reduced vision, slower reaction times, and mobility limitations. Door detection helps by:

  • Providing advance warning of doors to prepare for opening
  • Identifying automatic vs. manual doors
  • Detecting door swing direction to avoid collisions
  • Highlighting accessible entrances with ramps or automatic openers

3. Additional Beneficiaries

Beyond the primary user groups, door detection technology benefits:

  • Delivery robots: Autonomous robots navigating buildings
  • AR applications: Augmented reality wayfinding overlays
  • Building management: Automated door monitoring and maintenance
  • Emergency responders: Rapid building layout understanding

The Mobile Context

Why focus on mobile devices specifically? Smartphones offer several advantages for door detection:

üì± Mobile Advantages

  • Ubiquity: Most users already own smartphones
  • Sensors: Built-in cameras, accelerometers, gyroscopes
  • Feedback: Audio, haptic, and visual output capabilities
  • Portability: Always available, hands-free mounting options
  • Connectivity: Can access cloud services for heavy computation
  • Cost: No additional hardware required

However, mobile deployment also introduces constraints: limited computational power, battery life, real-time processing requirements, and varying lighting conditions [3].

Problem Statement

The core problem can be formally stated as:

Problem Formulation

Input: RGB image stream from mobile camera (optionally with depth data)
Output: Door locations (bounding boxes), door states (open/closed), door types (automatic/manual), and confidence scores
Constraints: Real-time processing (<100ms latency), mobile hardware, varying lighting, diverse door types
Success Criteria: >90% detection accuracy, <5% false positive rate, robust to occlusions and reflections

Key Takeaways

  • Doors are essential landmarks for indoor navigation
  • Low-vision users and seniors are primary beneficiaries
  • Mobile devices offer ubiquitous, cost-effective deployment
  • Real-time, accurate detection is critical for user safety
  • The problem requires balancing accuracy with computational efficiency
